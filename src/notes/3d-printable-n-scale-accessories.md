## A Tiny Layout with Big Ambitions

For the past few years, I've been working on a small N scale model train layout. It lives in a custom-built coffee table, right in the middle of my living room – which makes it both a layout and a conversation piece. If you're curious, you can find photos and updates on Instagram under @spur.n.couchtisch (translates to
*n.scale.coffeetable*).

As much fun as building this miniature world has been, one recurring issue kept bothering me: small accessory parts.

## The Problem with Accessories

Let's be honest—N scale is already a niche, and when it comes to scenery accessories, the selection has traditionally been... let's say "limited." Recently, things have started to change. More designers and manufacturers are offering detailed parts, and some of them are genuinely fantastic.

But there's a catch: price. Paying several euros for a single fence or a tiny trash container might make sense for one or two standout pieces, but not when you need dozens of them to populate your layout realistically.

So I figured: why not try designing and printing my own?

## Designing My Own

Armed with my 3D printer and a very humble level of CAD experience, I started creating a few simple parts. The first attempts were basic: some fences, highway barriers, a few simple props. Nothing fancy, but good enough to print and use.

Over time, I began expanding the collection—adding a German-style bottle bank, a Euro pallet, concrete barriers… Small everyday things that add a touch of realism to any layout when placed thoughtfully.

## Challenges When Designing for N Scale

Designing for N scale isn't just a matter of shrinking down a real object. It's a game of trade-offs between what looks right, what prints reliably, and what survives when handled.

Since I design everything for FDM printing, I try to avoid the need for support structures. That means keeping overhangs to a minimum, avoiding fragile vertical details, and ensuring that the base of the part sits flat on the build plate. It's like building tiny LEGO parts—except they have to survive both gravity and slicer logic.

Even with a 0.25 mm nozzle, detail is a constant struggle. You end up exaggerating features just enough for them to be visible, while trying not to make the model look like a caricature of itself.

Of course, resin printing would solve a lot of these issues. The level of detail you can achieve with a resin printer is remarkable. But that's not the goal here. I want my models to be something anyone can print at home. Without special equipment, expensive resins, or safety gear. FDM printing might be less detailed, but it's accessible. And let's be real: handling resin in your kitchen or living room isn't for everyone. The fumes alone are reason enough to stick with FDM.

## Current Work in Progress

Right now, I'm working on a series of cable spools in different styles and sizes. They're great little scenery elements—perfect for industrial areas, storage yards, or as train cargo. There's something satisfying about designing everyday objects in miniature. The goal is always the same: to add just enough detail to make them recognizable and believable—without pushing your printer (or your patience) too far.
